Life history evolution, species differences, and phenotypic plasticity in hemiparasitic eyebrights (Euphrasia)

Max R. Brown1, Natacha Frachon2, Edgar L.Y. Wong3, Chris Metherell4, and Alex D. Twyford1,2

  • 1University of Edinburgh, King's Buildings, Institute of Evolutionary Biology
  • 2Royal Botanic Garden Edinburgh
  • 3Department of Plant Sciences, University of Oxford
  • 4Botanical Society of Britain and Ireland
Max Brown, 20.03.2020

Euphrasia or eyebrights are parasitic plants in the family Orobanchaceae, which also contains other well known plant species such as Yellow Rattle and the Broomrapes. Euphrasia are notorious in botanical circles for being difficult to identify. This is because the species are recently diverged, often hybridising with one another and change their physical characteristics depending on which host they parasitise.

Our paper investigates different traits of the hemiparasitic Euphrasia when they parasitise different host plants. This was possible by growing many Euphrasia plants on different hosts in a common garden experiment. We also grow multiple Euphrasia species on a single host in a common garden experiment. In this way, we hope to remove any environmental variation and concentrate on host effects on parasitic Euphrasia and on the differences between Euphrasia species. Overall, we show that Euphrasia show variation in life history strategies both in response to host species and due to differences between Euphrasia species themselves. Some will rapidly transition to flower, while others delay flowering and invest in vegetative growth.

Phenotypic plasticity experiment:

In the first experiment, we used a single Euphrasia species (Euphrasia arctica) grown on eight hosts and also grown without a host (Figure 1). The main take home is that host species makes a large difference on several aspects of the biology of E. arctica. For example E. arctica grown on clover host (on the far right in the graph below) produces substantially taller plants (height), with larger flowers (corolla length), that also flower earlier (Julian days to flower). On the other hand, E. arctica plants without a host flowered 52(!) days later, were extremely short at first flowering and produced tiny flowers. Check out the traits on the graph below!

These differences are interesting because firstly they show that E. arctica can grow on a range of hosts, indicating that it is a generalist parasite. Secondly, which host a E. arctica plant parasitises has an impact on when the plant will flower and how large this plant will eventually be. These traits are correlated with reproductive success. Lastly, the trait 'nodes to flower', which is an important trait in discriminating between species of eyebright appears to be remarkably consistent across all the hosts we tested. This trait therefore might be valuable and reliable in telling species apart.

Species differences experiment:

We also ran an experiment in parallel which investigated the differences between species of Euphrasia when grown on a single host -- here we used clover (Figure 2). There is substantial variation in morphology between species in most traits measured. One of the species with a distinct life history strategy is E. micrantha which flowers from a low node while it is short. At the opposite end of the spectrum is E. pseudokerneri which flowers once it has grown tall and from a high node. Several closely related species such as E. arctica, E. confusa and E. nemorosa differ only in a few traits. E. nemorosa flowers later than E. arctica and at a higher node but overlaps in many other traits.

Overall we see differences in life history, which is consistent with the classic trade off between growth and reproduction. In addition, flower size represents another axis of variation across the species. Differences in flower size can have appreciable influence on the genetic variability of the species; large flowered species generally outcross and small flowered species generally inbreed.

Comparison of common garden and wild collected plants:

In addition to the common garden experiments (above) we were also sent many herbarium specimens from all over the country as part of the 'Eye for Eyebright' scheme that Alex Twyford initiated. We used these collections to see if the wild collected species correlated in their trait values with the same species grown in the common garden (Figure 3). For some traits (e.g. nodes to flower and corolla length), the correlations are strong, which indicates that our common garden results generalise to observations in nature. As expected, plants grown in the common garden were overall larger plants.

Figure 3: The relationship between morphological trait measurements made in the common garden and on wild collected herbarium specimens for diverse Euphrasia species. Each point represents a joint mean value, and error bars are standard errors of the mean.
