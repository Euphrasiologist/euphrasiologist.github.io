Host driven effects in Eyebrights

Using Eyebrights (Euphrasia) as our system of interest, we investigate how host species affect different aspects of parasitic Euphrasia plants. As Euphrasia plants are annual plants with a rapid life-cycle, excellent proxies for fitness can be obtained relatively easily. How host species affect Euphrasia fitness is of central importance in understanding whether host specialisation exists in the genus, and if it is affected by phylogenetic relatedness of host species. Extending this framework, do different species of Euphrasia specialise on different hosts? We also measure phenotypic and taxonomic traits to understand different life history strategies and to see whether traits in the common garden are consistent with current taxonomic circumscription in this critical genus.

Hybridisation across large scales in plants

With the advent of the Hybrid Flora of the British Isles (Stace et al., 2015), a now near complete catalogue of all hybrid plants ever detected in the British Isles has been compiled. These hybrids are interesting from an evolutionary perspective as each event has a wide arrange of possible outcomes, all the way from dead ends (infertile, unfit plants) to the bringing together or introgression of alleles from one plant species to another, which can potentially be of adaptive advantage. Our work is focused in two areas (1) on putting plant hybridisation in a rigorous phylogenetic context where quantative statements can be made with confidence across a large geographical area like the British Isles, and (2) looking at the prevalence of cross ploidy hybridisation in plants.

Genomic signatures of hybridisation

Two species of Eyebright in the UK are purported to be hybrid species that originated from a diploid-tetraploid hybridisation event. In light of this hypothesis, based on morphology and some cytogenetics from the 1950's we aim to first see whether there is any evidence of introgression across a ploidy level in Euphrasia in the wild. We also have whole genome sequences for both Euphrasia rivularis, Euphrasia vigursii and the parental species to understand hybridisation in detail across the genome.
